1. The 4th-Order Bending Equation

The "wiggling" of the arrow is not a simple sine wave. Because an arrow in flight is a continuous elastic structure with no external tension (unlike a guitar string), its internal bending is governed by the 4th-order Euler-Bernoulli beam equation (which also emerges as the effective field theory of a 1D crystalline membrane in quantum mechanics):

$$ \rho A \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} + \kappa \frac{\partial^4 y}{\partial x^4} = 0 $$

Where \( \rho A \) is the linear mass density, \( \kappa \) is the flexural rigidity (stiffness/spine), and \( y(x,t) \) is the lateral displacement. The 4th-order spatial derivative \( \partial^4 / \partial x^4 \) dictates that higher frequency modes require exponentially massive amounts of energy to excite, which is why we primarily only see the fundamental 1st mode (the Archer's Paradox wiggle) with the naked eye.

2. The Spatial Shape & The Nodes

Once the arrow leaves the bow, it experiences "Free-Free" boundary conditions (no shear force or bending moment at either tip). Solving the spatial part of the PDE yields the mode shape \( \phi(x) \):

$$ \phi_n(x) = \cos(\beta_n x) + \cosh(\beta_n x) - \sigma_n \left( \sin(\beta_n x) + \sinh(\beta_n x) \right) $$

For the fundamental mode (\( n=1 \)), the roots of the characteristic equation give \( \beta_1 L \approx 4.73004 \) and \( \sigma_1 \approx 0.9825 \). If you graph this equation, it crosses the zero-axis at exactly two points: x ≈ 0.224L and x ≈ 0.776L. These are the Stationary Nodes (marked in Red in the simulation). No matter how violently the arrow wiggles, these points never deviate from the center path!

3. Aerodynamic Stabilization (The Illusion)

While the Center of Mass travels in a perfectly straight line, the tail kicks out wildly. To fix this, the fletching (feathers) creates aerodynamic drag, acting as a damper on the oscillating system. We model the time-dependent amplitude \( A(t) \) of the wiggle as an underdamped harmonic oscillator:

$$ A(t) = A_0 e^{-\gamma t} \cos(\omega t) $$

Where \( A_0 \) is the initial tail kick force, \( \gamma \) is the aerodynamic damping coefficient (controlled by the slider), and \( \omega \) is the natural frequency of the arrow. As \( t \) increases, the exponential decay \( e^{-\gamma t} \) forces the wiggle to collapse, pulling the highly visible tail back into a straight line with the target.
